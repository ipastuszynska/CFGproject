Vienna

To Do

To Eat

Mozart’s Residence is the only remaining Viennese residence reopened as a museum on the day of his 250th birthday. Mozart lived on the first floor from 1784 to 1787, reputedly his happiest years in the city. The museum offers a thoroughly documented portrait of Mozart’s years in Vienna, with numerous drawings and paintings of the clan, original sheet music, letters and musical instruments.

Vienna's Flea Market is the largest open-air food market. Admire the stalls’ bounty, from buckets of tulips to pickles, wines and plump, purple-flushed figs, stopping for breakfast at Do-An or Tewa. On Saturdays, it’s worth getting here at 7am or earlier to clock a bargain. Alongside the market, look out for the lovely Otto Wagner Houses embossed with vines and flowers.

Riesenrad is a giant ferris wheel that features in ‘The Third Man’. It’s the only remaining work of British engineer Walter Basset who also built wheels for Blackpool, London and Paris. It was completed in 1897 to commemorate the Golden Jubilee of Franz Josef. A full circle in one of the 15 wooden gondolas takes a gentle 20 minutes, and at its highest point you are 65 metres up.

Steirereck im Stadtpark The undisputed star amongst Austria's gastronomic elite is Steirereck's astute owner-chef Heinz Reitbauer. Using local and organic products, Reitbauer assembles refined and unique dishes that are so visually appealing they could be framed as art works – almost a pity to eat. Menus change regularly and depend on availability and quality of fresh seasonal components.

Gasthaus Pöschl Located on Vienna's prettiest cobble-stoned square, Franziskanerplatz, this pub-like restaurant serves straight-forward, delectable Viennese cooking, to a mixed clientele. If the restaurant is packed there are seats outside. Friendly opening times and good hearty cooking attract locals and tourists alike.

Zur Eisernen Zeit is a etro-establishment serves what many say is the best beef goulash in Vienna. The menu is small but full of local specialities and includes the ubiquitous Schnitzel (pork or veal). Step back in time to experience a typically old-fashioned Viennese Gasthaus, where you can share a table, chat with the locals and enjoy good home cooking.
